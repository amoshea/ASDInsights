Behaviors
1. Social Interaction:

Social interaction refers to the skills and behaviors involved in communication and participation in social activities with others. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), social interaction can be challenging due to difficulties in understanding social cues, emotional reciprocity, and sharing skills. Monitoring social interaction involves observing the quality and quantity of interactions, as well as the use of appropriate social skills.

2. Anxiety:

Anxiety is an emotional response characterized by feelings of apprehension, restlessness, and worry. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), anxiety is commonly observed and may be related to difficulties in emotional regulation, social skills, and changes in routine. Monitoring anxiety allows for the identification of triggers and levels of anxiety, enabling the development of appropriate intervention strategies to reduce anxiety and improve well-being.

3. Pleasantness:

Pleasant behavior is characterized by positive attitudes and cooperative, respectful, and friendly behaviors. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), encouraging and reinforcing pleasant behaviors is essential to promote positive social interactions and the establishment of healthy relationships with others.

4. Impatience:

Impatience refers to a difficulty in dealing with situations that involve waiting, delays, or frustration. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), impatience can be influenced by difficulties in emotional self-regulation and tolerance for changes. Monitoring this behavior allows for the identification of triggering situations and the development of strategies for managing frustration and wait tolerance.

5. Aggression:

Aggression refers to hostile or violent behaviors directed towards oneself or others. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), aggression may arise due to difficulties in communication, frustration, or difficulties in emotional control. Monitoring aggression is crucial to identify patterns, potential triggering factors, and develop intervention strategies to promote the safety and well-being of the child and others.

6. Friendliness:

Being friendly involves the ability to establish and maintain positive social relationships, demonstrating friendly behaviors such as sharing, collaborating, and respecting others. Monitoring this behavior allows for the assessment of social skills development and identification of areas that require additional support to promote healthy social interaction.

7. Communication:

Communication encompasses all forms of expression and understanding of information, including speech, sign language, alternative communication, and pragmatic skills. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), communication can be affected by language difficulties, emotional expression, and understanding social cues. Monitoring the use of speech, sign language, or alternative communication can provide information about communication development and help adapt intervention strategies.

8. Concentration:

Concentration refers to the ability to direct and maintain focus on a specific task, blocking external and internal distractions. In children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), difficulties in concentration can occur due to issues such as hyper or hypo responsiveness to sensory stimuli, restricted interest in certain subjects, and difficulties in filtering stimuli. Monitoring concentration involves observing the child's ability to maintain focus on specific activities, as well as identifying factors that may interfere with concentration, such as excessive noise or intense visual stimuli. This information can help adapt the environment and support strategies to optimize the child's concentration and engagement in academic and social activities.
